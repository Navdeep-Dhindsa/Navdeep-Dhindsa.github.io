About me

I am a postdoctoral researcher at IMSc Chennai working broadly on Lattice QCD with Dr. M Padmanath. I did my PhD at IISER Mohali under the supervision of Dr. Anosh Joseph. The main theme of my PhD was about the non-perturbative studies of supersymmetric theories using the lattice framework. The calculations were performed for various dimensional theories using Monte Carlo with the help of computations in the MPI framework. In addition to lattice investigations, I am keenly interested in exploring other non-perturbative methods for studying strongly interacting field theories, with a broad focus on computational algorithms and their applications in this domain.

I come from Langrian (30°28'19.7"N, 75°59'28.1"E), a village in Punjab, India, where I completed my schooling. I completed my Bachelor's degree at Arya College, Ludhiana (affiliated with Panjab University, Chandigarh) and my Master's degree at Punjabi University, Patiala, where my genuine interest in research began.

Research Interests

My primary research focuses on investigating strong interactions in particle physics, with a key emphasis on employing lattice gauge theory as a fundamental methodology. As a newly appointed Postdoc, my current work involves the numerical study of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) using lattice techniques. Specifically, my research aims to conduct lattice QCD studies on hadrons composed predominantly of heavy quarks, exploring the potential for the presence of bound states in these heavy hadrons that can be experimentally measured.

I have expertise in working with the MILC-based SUSY LATTICE code, which utilizes the MPI framework. In the future, I plan to learn and implement the Chroma package, further enhancing my experience with MILC codes.

During my doctoral research, I focused on using the lattice framework for non-perturbative studies of field theories through numerical computations. This work has given me extensive experience with a variety of lattice techniques that are widely applicable. Studying field theories on a spacetime lattice is especially interesting because it provides insights into coupling regimes that perturbation theory cannot reach.

My initial research examined non-perturbative supersymmetry breaking in lower dimensions. This then expanded to include supersymmetric gauge theories and matrix models, with a focus on their holographic duals via gauge/gravity duality. Currently, I am interested in studying maximally supersymmetric theories on the lattice, particularly in one dimension, focusing on the BFSS model and its mass-deformed BMN model.

Another area of interest for me is addressing challenges in lattice field theories, such as the sign problem. While I have experience with Monte Carlo simulations, I am also familiar with other numerical tools like the complex Langevin method (CLM) and Tensor Networks (TN). However, I prefer enhancing Monte Carlo simulations in real-world dimensions, as generalizing CLM and TN in higher dimensions can be quite complex. Nevertheless, I remain open to using these other methods if they are better suited to solving specific problems. I typically use Bash and Python to analyze the data generated in my projects.

I'm also exploring non-lattice non-perturbative methods as alternatives to Monte Carlo or evolution methods, which often require substantial computational resources for adequate results. Recently, I've begun investigating the numerical bootstrap method for multi-matrix models. While generalizing this approach to more matrices presents challenges, it shows promise for quick convergence in the absence of analytic solutions.

Beyond the listed interests, I am eager to explore emerging approaches in lattice field theory, including the application of Machine Learning to train fields and the potential of Quantum Computing for studying Hamiltonian time evolution without concerns about simulation challenges like the sign problem.
